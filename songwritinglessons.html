Songwriting Lessons with Randy Bergida in Greenpoint, Brooklyn

The first thing I did when I started playing guitar was write songs. At first, they were just these little instrumental "compositions" that I intentionally wrote a bit too hard for myself to play. I started doing this around age nine or ten.

My curiosity was so strong that it eventually led me to the University of Arizona, where I decided to major in music with a focus on composition and guitar. In those early days, I remember having this overarching question about my writing: "Am I doing this right?"

Fast forward to my philosophy today: if it’s true, honest, you like it, and people are gravitating toward it, you are definitely doing it right. It’s art. If the artist believes in their work, then I believe it’s right.

Breaking Down Music Theory—Simply

Back in my university days, I realized that as a guitarist, I naturally knew a lot of theory, but the school was definitely more classically leaning. I came from a blues, rock, singer-songwriter, and pop background, which felt a bit intimidating at first.

But I learned traditional theory front to back and soaked it up like a sunny day at Jacob Riis. I realized I just had a deep love for music theory. It came naturally to me, and I felt I could break down concepts that textbooks made seem incredibly complex into something simple.

I love being able to blow my students' minds with the simplicity of theory and how it relates to songwriting. Ultimately, you want to write what you hear. But if you naturally weave yourself into a hole, you can use music theory to get you back to the edge of the pool. If you want to find your way up to the high dive and your ear is failing you, theory lets you try out ideas to see what sticks.

Making Melodies Move

And then there’s melody. Ah, what a fun thing to play with.

In the beginning, I remember my melodies could be just one note because the lyrics felt right. But after years of working closely with my good friend who is a cellist—collaborating on intimate nylon-string guitar and cello projects—I learned to think of melody as it would be heard on a string instrument.

If it’s just one note with interesting phrasing, yes, that can work. But if you start to move that melody around, the magic begins. I find a lot of my students who play an instrument and are trying to sync it with their voice have that same single-note tendency. It is so fun to push them into making that melody move.

The Personal Journey of Lyrics

And, of course, lyrics. The ever-so-personal lyrics. The words that bring people to tears, give them goosebumps, or make them feel ecstatic, cheerful, sad, or somber.

How one gets there is a personal journey. Some people have been writing since they could pick up a pen and it’s the most natural part of their day, while others find themselves going on a metaphorical vision quest.

The secret, like all things, is practicing and doing. I remember reading a quote—I believe it was Bob Dylan quoting Woody Guthrie—saying you have to write a hundred songs before you write a good one. Though I can attest that some people’s first songs are brilliant! Or at least, they reveal that there is a deep well of music just waiting to be released.
